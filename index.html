Motivation· Construct· Validation· Application· Discussion
Construct

SDM unfolds through six steps clustered into three non-interchangeable dimensions

Problem diagnosis → causal theorizing → hypothesis formulation → evidence collection → evidence evaluation → belief adjustment. Each dimension is necessary; none is substitutable.

Causal Reasoning (CR)
Steps 1–3  ·  11 items

Diagnosing a problem, constructing causal models, and formulating testable hypotheses. Foundational: without structured theory, evidence gathering becomes ad hoc.

Evidence Evaluation (EE)
Steps 4–5  ·  9 items

Design and conduct tests; appraise evidence against prior hypotheses; seek disconfirming signals. Quality of evidence collection determines feedback informativeness.

Adaptive Learning (AL)
Step 6  ·  4 items

Update beliefs proportionally to evidence; revise or abandon theories when disconfirming evidence accumulates. Distinct from EE: rigor in testing does not ensure belief updating.

Formative specification: dimensions define SDM rather than reflecting a single latent trait. Each is necessary and non-interchangeable — a manager can practice one without the others. Low inter-item correlations are expected and appropriate.

Validation · Criteria 4 & 5

Three dimensions are statistically distinct and SDM predicts decision quality

4 · Structural distinguishability

·
Why run CFA at all? EE and AL correlate highly (r = .95) — a natural question is whether they should be one dimension. We used CFA as a specification test to answer it: not to assess fit (which is expected to be poor for formative constructs), but to ask whether 3 factors are statistically distinguishable from 2.
·
Conceptually, they are non-interchangeable: a manager can evaluate evidence rigorously and still not update prior beliefs — motivated reasoning and escalation of commitment each illustrate this.
·
Statistically confirmed: constrained model (EE–AL fixed to 1) fits significantly worse — Δχ² = 131.86, Δdf = 1, p < .001.

5 · Behavioral prediction (vignette accuracy)

·
β = .12, p < .001 — stable across all 4 specifications (range .117–.122). Not confounded by tier, education, tenure, or decision area.
·
Top quintile (SDM ≥ 3.47): 54.7% correct  ·  Bottom quintile (SDM ≤ 2.96): 44.3% correct
Qualification: SDM and vignettes developed by the same team — circularity mitigated but not eliminable from the published record. Priming experiment (d = 2.09, independent sample) is the primary validity evidence.

Discussion · Limitations

What the evidence does not yet establish

Criterion
Same-team design — SDM scale and vignettes built by the same team. Circularity mitigated but not eliminable. External criterion validation is the priority next step.
Convergent
AOT and NFC not administered — the two closest established constructs (Actively Open-minded Thinking; Need for Cognition) were not collected in the deployment study. Proxies are consistent with theory but do not substitute. → App I: proposed fix
Design
Cross-sectional — for now, we cannot establish whether SDM predicts outcomes over time or whether the hierarchy gradient reflects selection, socialization, or experience.
Sample
Self-selected firms — organizations opted in; may compress the lower tail of the SDM distribution and attenuate between-firm variance.

Appendix B — Psychometric Details · Tables B.1–B.5

CFA specification tests — structural distinguishability

Poor CFA fit is diagnostic for formative constructs (indicators cause the construct; they are not expected to covary). The purpose of CFA here is not model fit but to test whether three factors are statistically distinct from two.

SpecificationCFIRMSEAInterpretation
1-factor (all items)< .50highConfirms indicators are not interchangeable reflections of one latent trait
3-factor, reflective< .50highExpected — formative specification misfit under reflective CFA
3-factor correlated (main)< .50highNote: CR × EE interfactor = −.88 (Heywood artifact); composite correlations all positive (Table B5)
2-factor (EE + AL merged)< .50highΔχ² vs. 3-factor = 131.86, Δdf = 1, p < .001 — EE and AL are statistically distinct
Key result
Despite high composite-score correlation between EE and AL (r = .95), the 3-factor model fits significantly better than the 2-factor model (Δχ² = 131.86, p < .001). The three dimensions are statistically distinguishable. Poor CFI does not undermine this conclusion — it confirms the formative specification.

Future work — Proposed convergent validity study

AOT and NFC — the missing nomological links

What these constructs are

AOT
Actively Open-minded Thinking (Baron, 1991). Willingness to seek information that could contradict current beliefs, to consider alternative viewpoints, and to revise conclusions when evidence warrants. Measured by AOT-7 (Haran et al., 2013) or the 41-item full scale. Directly maps to SDM's Adaptive Learning dimension.
NFC
Need for Cognition (Cacioppo & Petty, 1982). Intrinsic motivation to engage in and enjoy effortful analytical thinking. High-NFC individuals seek complex problems and process information systematically. Measured by NFC-6 (Lins de Holanda Coelho et al., 2020). Maps to SDM's Causal Reasoning dimension.

Why we expect moderate, not high, correlations

SDM measures the structured practice of scientific reasoning in decision contexts. AOT and NFC measure dispositions — openness and motivation. A high-NFC manager may think hard but not scientifically. A high-AOT manager may update beliefs but without structured hypothesis testing. SDM requires the specific methodology.

r ≈ .20–.40
Expected range · convergent without collapsing construct distinctiveness
r > .60
Would indicate SDM is redundant with AOT/NFC · not expected
